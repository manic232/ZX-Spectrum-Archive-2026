The Toy Box

The games I remember playing as a child in the 80s.

The Toy Box… an old wooden box my Grandpa made for me when I was very young. I probably kept toys in it at some point, but by the time I had my Spectrum in the 80s it had a far more important job: it became the platform where my Spectrum tapes lived — including the ones I’d copied using my Dad’s hi-fi system. They would sit on top of the box, lined up and waiting to be played. I still have the Toy Box today.

I only ever owned a 48K Spectrum back then, so there are no 128K titles here. To me, the 128K versions were essentially the same games, just with better sound thanks to the AY-3-8912 chip.

I’ve tried to list as many of the games as I can remember playing. It’s probably not complete — there are bound to be a few I’ve forgotten over the years.

Where possible, the games link to their CRASH reviews (shown in white). If no CRASH review exists, I’ve used my original instructions instead (shown in red).

 

My Memories

I had my Spectrum perched on top of an old chest of drawers — no desk, just whatever space I could claim. I’d pick a game from the Toy Box lid, pop the tape into the deck, type LOAD"", and then wait the obligatory two or three minutes, buzzing with anticipation about the wonders that were surely about to appear… only for the thing to crash with the dreaded R Tape loading error, 0:1.

In the early days I only had a black-and-white TV, the same one bought for me when I had my ZX81. My parents kept a portable colour TV in our dining room, and I used to pinch it at weekends. Every Sunday night my mum would shout up the stairs, “BRING THAT TV DOWN NOW!” Sometimes I did… but more often than not I “forgot.” Eventually I won the battle and it stayed in my room permanently.

My grandmother — we called her Muz or Muzzy — would sometimes come into my room after I told her about a new game I’d bought. She’d sit there watching as I excitedly showed her how it worked. I doubt she was even remotely interested, but she pretended for my sake, and that meant everything.

We had endless trouble with our first tape deck. We even took it back to Boots to get it replaced. Looking back, it was probably fine — just the usual Spectrum tape-loading foolishness. Not long after I got my Spectrum, we visited my cousin Justin (who also had one) so he could show me the ropes. I remember him loading up some of his games — one was River Rescue, which I still love to this day.

Friends and family often played my games too. I remember my cousins Grant and Damien staying with us once, and Grant became completely absorbed in The Wild Bunch. He sat there for ages trying to work out who the culprit was, making detailed notes in an attempt to beat it. Years later I told him this story… and he had absolutely no memory of it.

One thing that amazed me as a kid was when games included in-game speech. Usually it was on the title screen or after completing a level. I remember Ghostbusters, Robin of the Wood, and Splat! having speech, and I remember listening in total amazement, convinced it had to be some kind of sorcery. What devilment was involved here? Was it simply clever use of the BEEP command? An early sampling method? Witchcraft? Who knows — but I found it magical.

The games I remember playing most were Daley Thompson's Decathlon, River Rescue, Jet Set Willy, Manic Miner, Splat!, Alien 8, Knight Lore, Underwurlde, Wheelie and 3D Deathchase. I have a vivid memory of having a dentist appointment one morning before school and loading up 3D Deathchase for a quick game beforehand — presumably to distract myself from the horrors waiting in the dentist’s chair.
