Welcome to my ZX Spectrum Archive reboot, new and updated in 2026. Best viewed with RetroZilla.

An awful lot has changed in the world of retro computing since I started these pages in 2001, vintage games are more popular now than they have ever been, with countless retro style consoles and computers being released in recent years. We had the Bluetooth Spectrum keyboard released in 2014, then in 2024 'The Spectrum' an emulated recreation of the original 48k machine by Retro Games Ltd, and now in 2026 we have the Kick Starter 3 version of the Spectrum Next by Henrique Olifiers, an FPGA reimplementation of the Spectrum hardware, that does so much more than the original could have ever dreamed of.  When will it end? who knows, but the inherent nostalgia that seems to exist in our DNA has made retro gaming big business. This is why I wanted to finish and update this site, to finish what I so rudely abandoned in 2003.

The site was nearly complete when I abandoned it, but there were some game links to finish off in the CRASH Smash section, and I got bored with the laborious process of finding and processing each game's information.  The last game I completed was Popeye. In April 2026 I decided to not only finish what I had started but bring the page up to date.  Much has changed since 2003, I have made a ton of updates and refinements, the About section details these.

An Introduction to the Spectrum

By far the most popular and successful of his many products, the ZX Spectrum earned Clive Sinclair a fortune, a knighthood for "services to British industry" and a lasting place in the national consciousness. Huge numbers of Spectrums were sold around the world, making it by some way the most successful British computer ever made. Sinclair's standing rose so high that in 1983 Margaret Thatcher personally presented a Spectrum to the Japanese Prime Minister as a symbol of British technological prowess.

The Spectrum was the longest-lived Sinclair product, eventually appearing in seven distinct versions produced over a six-year period. Some information on the various models are linked below. I have not included any info on the QL or any of the many clone systems, but have added a page for the Spectrum Next for this 2026 reboot.

The +2, +2A and +3 were produced by Amstrad following its 1986 buy-out of Sinclair's computer business. As well as all of these different product versions, no less than thirteen different versions of the basic hardware appeared during the six years that the Spectrum was produced. The Spectrum 48K Versions and Spectrum 128K Versions pages detail these.

The Spectrum continued to sell into the early 1990s, but by about 1992 it had been squeezed out by the more advanced 16-bit computers and the cheap but more capable Sega and Nintendo games consoles.

A licensed clone of the ZX Spectrum, the TS 2068, was launched in the United States by Timex in late 1983.  However, the machine's simplicity meant that it was easy to copy and many illegal clones were produced elsewhere in the world. Spectrum clones were produced in many Eastern European and developing countries, including the USSR, Czechoslovakia, Romania, Hong Kong, Argentina and Brazil. Even today, cheap variants of the Spectrum continue to be produced in Russia.

The simple architecture of the Spectrum makes it easy to emulate. Spectrum emulators are available for almost any modern OS, smart phones and single board computers like the Raspberry Pi. Thousands of Spectrum games and programs have been converted to emulator friendly formats like TZX or TAP.

In 2017 the Spectrum Next was released, an updated and enhanced version of the original ZX Spectrum, compatible with all original software, featuring major hardware developments, and a beautiful case design created by the original Sinclair industrial designer Rick Dickinson, who sadly passed away in 2018.

Getting hold of a Spectrum is still relatively easy. There are large numbers in circulation - the standard second-hand prices in 2001 when I started this site were between around £20-£30 for the 48K Spectrum and £50 or so for the +2 and +3. I think those prices may have risen a tad in recent years!. The best place to find second-hand Spectrums remains eBay or these days AmiBay. The rarest versions are the 16K Spectrum and the Spectrum 128, which often fetched prices of upwards of £100 in 2001, I'd image they would be a lot more than that these days.
